In fact, as we can see, it is possible to move out to a better (smaller) objective contour passing through B. Even then we can move out to a still better objective contour passing through C. Points A and B represent what are known as local optima. Many computational procedures (such as separable programming) can guarantee no more than local optima. Clearly what is really required is a true global optimum such as that represented by point C in Figure 7.11. A fairly graphic way of describing the situation is to consider the problem of a team of mountaineers on a range of mountains in a thick fog. It is easy for them to determine when they are at a local optimum, i.e. a mountain peak. The ground will begin to drop in height whichever way they walk. This does not guarantee, however, that there is not another higher mountain peak hidden in the fog. The situation of the mountaineers is similar to that of many non-linear programming algorithms.
