Should a problem with a block angular structure have no common constraints it should be obvious that optimizing it simply amounts to optimizing each subproblem with its appropriate portion of the objective. For our simple numerical example if there had been no raw material constraint we could have solved each factory's model separately and obtained an overall company optimum. In fact as far as the company was concerned it would have been perfectly acceptable to treat each factory as autonomous. Once we introduce common constraints, however, this will probably no longer be the case, as the small example demonstrated. The more common constraints there are, the more interconnected the separate plants must be. In Section 4.2 we discuss how a knowledge of the optimal solutions to the subproblems might be used to obtain an optimal solution to the total problem.
