This model obviously has a very special structure to which we will refer later. Notice that we have included variables for non-allowed routes in the model with objective coefficients M (some very large number). This has been done simply to preserve the pattern of the model. In practice, if we were to solve the model as a linear programming problem of this form we would simply leave these variables out.
Constraints (2), (3), and (4) are known as availability constraints. There is one such constraint for each of the three suppliers. These constraints ensure that the total quantity out of a supplier (in a year) does not exceed his capacity. Constraints (5), (6), (7), and (8) are known as requirement constraints. These constraints ensure that each customer obtains his requirement. In some formulations of the transportation problem constraints (2), (3), and (4) are treated as = instead of <. If the sum total of the availabilities exactly matches the sum total of the requirements then this is acceptable since all capacities must obviously be completely exhausted. In a case such as our numerical examples, however, this is not so. Total capacity (284) exceeds total demand (275). This can be coped with by introducing a dummy customer T5 with a requirement for the excess of 9.
